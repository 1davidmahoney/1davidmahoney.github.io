Introduction

This project, the Superhero Puzzle System, was created to demonstrate my systems design skills and my familiarity with Unreal Engine. A playable demo can be found here.

In action-adventure games, you’re sometimes tasked with changing the state of some environmental object in order to proceed. For example, you might knock down a tree to create a bridge over a raging river. As a superhero, though, such tasks are often trivial, which begs the question: what’s the greatest challenge to all superheroes? Helpless civilians, of course!

In the absence of an adequate physical challenge, the player should at least be emotionally challenged. In the example level documented below, the Superhero Puzzle System tasks you with one such challenge: weighing the safety of numerous strangers against the safety of one person you care about.

Narrative setup:

  • The villain stands on the front deck of a ferry, seemingly prepared to kill your beloved companion.
  • As the city burns, panicked civilians swarm toward the ferry.
  • Do you take the time to methodically scare the civilians away, ensuring their safety, but risking your companion’s life? Or, do you hustle to aggressively scare away the civilians, better ensuring your companion’s safety, but risking the civilians’ lives?

As the city’s only superhero, the people count on you. Unfortunately, in order to reach the villain, your sense of responsibility must be tested. Can you live up to the expectations of being a superhero? Complete the puzzle, and find out!

System

In the example level, light poles line the dock on which panicked civilians are gathering. The player uses the Superhero Puzzle System to overcharge and ignite the poles’ light bulbs, scaring the civilians away from the villain while simultaneously clearing a path toward him.

The high-level challenge presented by the system is the identification of “good” versus “bad” poles under emotional and temporal constraints. Some poles are “sturdy,” able to be electrified until they burst into flames (“ignited”), thus scaring the civilians away toward safety. Other poles are “broken” and will fall apart upon ignition, possibly injuring nearby civilians and/or damaging nearby structures. Both pole types look deceptively similar, so the player must exercise caution to avoid mistakes.

The system allows you to play according to your mood and your personal assessment of priorities.

With each charge, electrical energy in the pole’s light bulb builds up and then dissipates. If you’re in the mood to play in a more carefree way, you can aggressively spam the “interact” button (5-6 rapid presses) and simply wait for the bulb to be overcharged. Or, if you want to be slick and minimize your input, you can methodically wait until each charge peaks before starting another (just 3 well-timed button presses).

Some poles are sturdy; some are broken. If you’re prioritizing your companion’s safety, you might use the system aggressively, with reckless abandon, as long as you reach your goal with haste. Or, if you’re prioritizing virtually everyone’s safety, you might take some time to methodically test the limitations of the system, looking for ways to help more people.

Development

One of the biggest challenges in building the system was the “charge” mechanic. Though the Timeline component is a handy feature of Unreal, it proved to be troublesome in this case because of its limitations. To have more control, I realized I’d have to take a more manual approach for easing between values (e.g., lights fading up and down). Fortunately, because more data was exposed, this manual approach made it easier to tune the system in script (Blueprints) and to set it up for in-editor tuning by a level designer (via public variables).

Another big development challenge was implementing the “broken” pole. Unlike the “sturdy” pole, the “broken” type is rather complex, involving a few unique features: a detachable bulb object; optional “pole destructible” objects which react to broken poles; and a number of ways for civilians to behave, depending on their proximity to the bulb and the presence or absence of a “pole destructible” object.

Conclusion

By offering complementary puzzle pieces – in this case, risky poles, vulnerable civilians, and a helpless companion – the Superhero Action System allows the creation of dynamic and emotional puzzles in a world that feels both fantastical and grounded.

Beyond the example level presented here, the system could also be used in other emotionally rich yet superhuman contexts. For example, the light poles could be replaced with stubborn containers you pry open in order to attract people, rather than repel them; or you might work at pulling a rope to lift up an elevator full of innocent bystanders.

In its original incarnation, Telltale Games was a fast-paced studio, so it was understandable that priorities could shift rapidly and that you’d often be expected to solve your own problems. Fortunately, I was able to turn a few of the big challenges I came across into fun side-projects.

Now that I’ve moved on from Telltale, it’s been fun thinking of other exciting ways to explore the world of dialog and narrative—an example of which can be seen in the third project below.

Subtitle Pass Assistant

This tool was all about saving clicks and keypresses.

For the CDA, dialog-related work was a daily occurrence, but when it came time for a certain process that occurred a couple times per episode, the work became needlessly cumbersome because of a lack of tools specific to the task. After consulting with senior engineering staff, I set about solving this efficiency problem by writing a tool of my own in C++.

More specifically, my program made use of HTML-embedded JavaScript and automated mouse clicks and button-presses to rapidly operate and transfer data between Telltale’s proprietary tool, a web browser, and the operating system’s clipboard.

Using my program, I was happy to save myself and my fellow CDAs somewhere around 75% of the work previously required to complete this task.

File Management System for VO Processing

Like the program above, my file management system was created to address a specific problem I had encountered while working as a CDA.

When it came time to add special effects to voiceover (e.g., distortion when talking through a walkie-talkie), the CDA would receive a list of VO audio files to move to a specific folder, making them readily available for the Audio department. Like before, I found myself wondering if this task could be easier; and thanks to batch scripting, it was.

By combining a batch script, specially tagged lines of dialog, and existing functionality within Telltale’s proprietary tool, I was able to design a system which streamlined the process of identifying and relocating VO files in need of special effects—an improvement which eased workloads for myself and at least a few of my coworkers.

In addition to the satisfaction of having designed a useful system, it was great having the chance to collaborate with like-minded individuals in the Production and Audio departments toward solving a problem that had regularly affected us all.

Natural NPC Interaction System

This theoretical system is meant to bridge the experientially disruptive gap that games sometimes insert between the player’s button press and the NPC’s behavior.

In many adventure-type games, when interacting with an NPC, I’ve often been jarred by a button-press that leads to an NPC’s action rather than my own. For example, The Last of Us handles dialog interactions fairly well by leading most of their resultant cinematic segments (i.e., acting) with a line/action from Joel (the player character, or “PC”); however, pressing the interaction button can also mean “Make Ellie talk,” which may make some players feel disconnected from the gameplay. In a more recent title, Horizon Zero Dawn, the transition into cinematic segments can be even more jarring, as pressing the interaction button could mean “Cut to a completely new camera angle; and also make the NPC talk.”

To alleviate this issue, I’ve designed a slightly more complex, yet significantly more natural-feeling system—one modeled after the relatable concepts of personal space and situational awareness. With this system, a game may have more intuitive interactions by providing a simple, reasonable explanation as to why their resultant cinematics are led by the PC or by the NPC.

Every NPC has an “awareness zone”; when the player enters it, interaction UI appears over the NPC’s head, indicating that the NPC is now aware of the player’s presence and is interactable—i.e., a button-press may now trigger a cinematic segment which is led by the PC speaking to / acting upon the NPC.

Additionally, each NPC has “personal space,” which is like the awareness zone, but with a tighter radius and a stronger—and possibly unexpected—reaction. Upon entering an NPC’s personal space, the player has a short amount of time to press the interaction button before the NPC will react to the player’s proximity; in general, happy NPCs allow more time, whereas angry NPCs allow less time. The activation of this new mode of interaction is made clear by an accented button prompt that fades out over a few seconds.

Altogether, there are 3 ways to begin an interaction with an NPC... ...each with its own unique outcome.
A) Enter the NPC’S awareness zone & interact. PC leads a cinematic segment.
B) Enter the NPC’S personal space & interact. PC leads a cinematic segment.
C) Enter the NPC’S personal space & wait. NPC leads a cinematic segment.

For example, if the NPC is a hard-nosed, rough-around-the-edges type of character...

Scenario Cinematic start Cinematic end
A) Awareness zone + interact PC greets NPC. NPC responds gruffly.
B) Personal space + interact PC taps NPC on shoulder. NPC threatens you.
C) Personal space + wait PC stands silently for a moment. NPC jokes about you being sneaky.

Conclusion

As next steps, I’d like to implement this experimental dialog system in Unreal Engine and further develop the concept.

Introduction

Throughout college, I spent a lot of time prototyping mechanics for classes and for fun. My appreciation for the intuitive and accessible nature of smartphones led to these prototypes being largely focused toward mobile devices. Below are a few of my favorite ventures into mobile game design.

PolyVertex

Summary

PolyVertex was inspired by my time learning to model in Maya. Manipulate a polygon into matching a specified pattern by dragging vertices and cutting faces.

Development

This Flash-based game is my favorite mobile prototype for a few reasons, one of the biggest being the technical hurdles I had to overcome. Confronted with the need to script with raw trigonometry, I was intimidated by the challenge but excited to tackle it. Thankfully, the effort was worth it, as I learned valuable new skills along the way and ended up with a new and interesting style of game.

Tabs

Summary

Tabs is meant to feel like a real-world, handheld puzzle. In each round of gameplay, you’re faced with a jumbled configuration of tabs which must be set right. To complete each round, you hold and drag each tab, rotating it about its pivot, trying to get its other end to land on a target.

Development

Because Tabs is physics-based, I knew development would be a challenge, so I went through a few prototyping phases to ensure I was heading toward an end-product that would work well and feel good. The first prototype was physical, using paper, cardboard, and push-pins to approximate my original vision for the game.

With this real-world artifact, I was able to assess the game idea’s viability as an entertainment product, and to then begin the documentation process in earnest, which in turn allowed me to explore new mechanics and puzzle configurations.

Once satisfied with the direction of the design on paper (and cardboard and Scotch tape), I built digital prototypes in Corona and Flash, where I continued to experiment with the visual style.

Bomb Defuser

Summary

Bomb Defuser tasks you with quickly arranging tiles to match a specified pattern. Fail three times, and the device explodes! Gameplay consists of dragging rows and columns of symbols into proper alignment. It sounds simple, but with the right difficulty parameters, gameplay may become quite intense and engaging.

Development

The Bomb Defuser prototype was created in Flash and deployed on iPhone and iPad for testing.

The biggest challenge was the line-drawing system. Each target pattern (in the small window at the bottom of the screen) is randomized, as are the lines connecting the pattern’s tiles. Furthermore, once the pattern is matched in the play area, lines grow from the play area’s tiles, matching the lines in the target pattern (see video above).

Bomb Defuser is one of my favorite prototypes because it shows my ability to deliver not just a fun mechanic, but a complete system that offers experiences ranging from tranquil (“super casual” difficulty settings) to exhilarating (“hardcore”).

Introduction

This writing sample is based on Marvel’s X-Men – specifically, Professor Xavier’s School for Gifted Youngsters, where students must acclimate to their new home while also coping with their strange powers, not to mention the usual trials and tribulations of being a teenager.

Aside from these details, everything else – characters, superpowers, etc. – is created specifically to fit the narrative below.

Overview

Two new teenage students at Xavier’s school have trouble fitting in and accepting their new, unfamiliar circumstances. Tommy struggles to prevent the past from defining him. Beck struggles with her fear of the unknown. Their insecurities trip them up at first, but over the course of the scene, the two teens slowly find strength in their newfound companionship.

Characters

Tommy, the main character, is passionate but unfocused. He means well but gets into trouble. Age: 16. Upbringing: strict. Superpower: light generation.

Beck, a supporting character, is outgoing but gullible. She makes her own rules, but she shields herself with routine. Age: 17. Upbringing: sheltered. Superpower: splitting molecules.

Setting

Dusk. A grassy clearing in the woods. The school, an old mansion, lies far in the distance.

Scene

EXT. SCHOOL GROUNDS – CLEARING

TOMMY, a teenaged boy wearing a school uniform, enters a clearing far from the mansion. He agitatedly swats tall reeds away from his face, then pauses for a breath. He looks back at the mansion and sighs heavily. He turns forward again, grimacing at the dreary path ahead.

TOMMY

(frustrated, to himself)

You came all the way out here for a reason, man!

TOMMY

It’s time to–

Tommy begrudgingly pulls out an antique pocket watch, but it’s becoming too dark outside to see well, so he balls his hand into a fist and then quickly fans out his fingers, causing a glowing ball of smoky light to form in mid-air, inches from his hand. The watch is beautiful. Tommy slowly moves his sphere of light, forcing its reflection to flow around the watch’s ornate etchings, mesmerizing himself in the process. He’s snapped back to reality as he turns the watch, revealing a crudely welded seam along the lid’s edge.

TOMMY

Friggin’ cavemen, I swear...

Tommy gazes once more at his beloved memento as he slowly extinguishes his light, then pockets the watch.

TOMMY

Sorry, Pop.

Tommy eyes the faint treetops pensively, then shakes his head with dissatisfaction.

TOMMY

[sigh]

Tommy turns and starts walking briskly back toward the mansion, as if to confront someone/something.

BECK

(aggressive)

Hey! Hold it!

A teenage girl, BECK, in an identical school uniform stands several yards ahead of Tommy, blocking his way to the school and pointing her hands at him in a threatening but awkward manner. Tommy is briefly alarmed, but he quickly collects himself, as if he were expecting an unpleasant surprise anyway.

BECK

What’re you doing out here?! You a spy or somethin’?!

TOMMY

(agitated)

Are you serious? I’m a student... We’re wearing the same uniform... We were at orientation together!

As Tommy rudely explains away Beck’s suspicion, she eases her offensive stance, instead looking a bit annoyed.

BECK

(sardonic)

Okay, not a spy. Just another grumpy-ass “gifted youngster.” Cool.

Tommy drops his shoulders for a beat, embarrassed at his rudeness.

TOMMY

(disappointed)

*Not* cool. Look, I don’t usually flip out like that. It’s just that–

Beck is disarmed, realizing Tommy’s idea of “flipping out” is pretty timid. Tommy humbly produces his pocket watch and gestures as if to offer a look to Beck, who begins calmly approaching.

BECK

(vaguely sympathetic)

Lemme guess. Bully broke your stuff?

Tommy gets lost in thought for a beat upon seeing the watch again, but collects himself for an actual answer.

TOMMY

(sarcastic)

Some *tough* guy welded it shut ‘cause I–

TOMMY

(downtrodden)

Guess I was just looking at it too much.

Beck reaches Tommy and puts her hand out to receive the watch, but Tommy awkwardly backs away a step.

TOMMY

(stalling)

Er...

BECK

(teasing)

Oh. Not big on sharing, huh?

TOMMY

(meek chuckle)

It’s just, uh...

BECK

It’s alright, dude. We’re all here ‘cause we don’t play well with others, right?

Tommy smiles faintly in appreciation and loosens up.

TOMMY

Right.

Their eye contact becomes awkward as they realize they’re actually “playing well” with each other. Beck nods toward the path behind Tommy.

BECK

So what changed your mind?

Tommy glances at the path behind him.

TOMMY

What, about leaving? How’d you know I was–

BECK

Bright ball of light in the middle of an open field... at night...? Kinda hard to miss.

Tommy shifts uncomfortably, realizing someone caught him being vulnerable. Beck glares back at the school in the distance.

BECK

Why go back to that dusty old heap, especially knowing you’re gonna run into your old pal?

TOMMY

(serious)

‘Cause I’m gonna make that guy answer for this.

Tommy pockets the watch again.

TOMMY

One way or another.

BECK

Props for stepping up. But if he mangled your stuff *that* badly...?

BECK

(half-joking)

Pretty sure you and that watch are just gonna get melted down.

TOMMY

(awkward)

Ha...

TOMMY

(sighs)

Yeah.

Beck jokingly feigns irritation and puts out her hand, as if expecting something. Tommy is skeptical, but she insists by gesturing toward the watch, which Tommy finally hands to her.

BECK

(teasing)

Don’t freak out, now.

Beck concentrates on the watch, then makes a wide circular motion with her free hand. She hands the watch back to Tommy, and he looks at it and her incredulously.

TOMMY

It’s still–

Beck smirks and taps on the lid, causing it to flip open.

TOMMY

(shocked)

You did it! I didn’t think... I mean... Thank you. Seriously.

BECK

Yeah, well, I don’t work for free, and it’s, uh...

Beck gestures at the darkness surrounding them. Tommy sheepishly pats his body as if searching for a missing wallet.

TOMMY

I, uh... didn’t pack much, so I don’t really...

BECK

(laughs)

No worries. Really just need a light for the return trip, y’know?

Tommy looks determinedly at the mansion, then smiles warmly at Beck.

TOMMY

You got it.

The pair begins walking through the darkening woods toward the mansion. Tommy ignites a light sphere again.

TOMMY

(lighthearted)

So, uh... Late-night spy-hunting... Is that a regular thing for you? Not that I’m judging or anything...

Beck looks slightly embarrassed.

BECK

Been running away every night for the past week.

BECK

But then it starts getting dark, and I’m actually kinda...

TOMMY

(gentle disbelief)

...afraid of the dark?

BECK

(stubborn)

I just have a good imagination, is all.

BECK

Speaking of which, got a few ideas about getting back at that stupid bully of yours.

TOMMY

(laughs)

You’re kinda twisted, huh?

BECK

(joking)

Just enough to get a room at *this* place, apparently.

TOMMY

(joking)

Guess I need to step it up a notch, then...

BECK

(half-serious)

Not too much, though, alright? Got enough weirdos running around this place as it is.

TOMMY

(laughs)

No promises.

Beck gives Tommy a mischievous grin, and the two continue into the dark woods toward the school, guided by Tommy’s smoky sphere of light.
